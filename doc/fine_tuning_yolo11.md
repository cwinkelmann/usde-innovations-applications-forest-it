# Best training datasets for Eikelboom-style aerial wildlife detection

**The Eikelboom benchmark is not actually drone imagery — it uses oblique photos from manned aircraft at 90–120 m over Kenyan savanna, detecting elephants, giraffes, and zebras across 561 images with ~10,000 bounding boxes.** For a YOLO11l fine-tuning pipeline targeting this benchmark, the strongest training candidates are the Liège African Mammals dataset (10,239 nadir UAV images of six savanna species), the MMLA dataset (811,000 YOLO-format annotations on Kenyan wildlife from low-altitude drones), and the Mara-Wildlife dataset from WildlifeMapper (77,000 tiled images covering 21 Kenyan savanna species). These three datasets collectively span the species, ecosystems, and aerial viewing geometries closest to Eikelboom's evaluation conditions while providing the volume needed for robust YOLO fine-tuning. The camera-trap-to-aerial domain gap is severe — studies show **20–50 point mAP drops** when deploying COCO-pretrained models on aerial wildlife data — making domain-specific training data essential rather than optional.

## The Eikelboom benchmark is oblique aerial, not nadir drone

A critical and widely misunderstood detail: the Eikelboom et al. (2019) dataset was captured from **manned light aircraft flying at 170–200 km/h**, not from drones. Published as "Improving the precision and accuracy of animal population estimates with aerial image object detection" in *Methods in Ecology and Evolution*, the images come from Kenya Wildlife Service aerial counts in the **Laikipia-Samburu Ecosystem, Kenya** (2014–2015). The camera view is **oblique** (side-facing from the aircraft), not nadir — a fundamental geometric difference from most UAV surveys.

Each image is **5,000 × 3,000 pixels** (RGB, ~15 MP). Animals appear at roughly **50 × 50 pixels** on average, described as "often poorly visible for the human eye." The dataset contains exactly three species-level classes — elephant (3,068 annotations), zebra (4,702), and giraffe (2,514) — with a fixed 393/56/112 train/val/test split. Annotations are CSV-format bounding boxes, requiring conversion for YOLO training. The original paper used RetinaNet with a ResNet backbone and achieved **mAP 0.77** on the test set, detecting 90–95% of animals found by human observers and even finding 2.8–4.0% of animals missed by all four human annotators.

The dataset is freely available at **4TU.ResearchData** (DOI: 10.4121/uuid:ba99a206-3e5a-4673-b830-b5c866445b8c). It is not hosted on LILA BC or Zenodo, though LILA lists it on their external datasets page. May et al. (2025) in *Ecological Informatics* directly benchmarked modern YOLOv8 and HerdNet models on this dataset (referenced as "JE-TL19"), finding that **modern YOLO-based detection is now competitive with localization models even on oblique imagery**, and that point annotations can replace bounding boxes with minimal counting accuracy loss.

## Five datasets form the optimal training poolin /data/mnt/storage/Datasets

Based on ecosystem match, viewing geometry, annotation format, species overlap, and accessibility, these datasets best serve a 3-phase YOLO11l fine-tuning pipeline targeting Eikelboom-style evaluation:

**Liège University African Mammals** is the single most valuable training resource. Created by Delplanque et al. (2022), it contains **10,239 nadir UAV images** with bounding boxes for six African savanna species: buffalo, elephant, kob, topi, warthog, and waterbuck. The imagery spans four African countries (DRC, Botswana, Namibia, South Africa) collected between 2014–2018, providing substantial habitat diversity. It shares two species with Eikelboom (elephant and buffalo as a proxy for large ungulates) and its multi-country provenance helps generalization. Available from the ULiège Dataverse with a 6,962/978/2,299 train/val/test split, it requires CSV-to-YOLO format conversion. Delplanque's Libra-RCNN achieved **mAP 0.80** on this data, surpassing Eikelboom's original RetinaNet results.

**MMLA (Multi-Environment, Multi-Species, Low-Altitude)** is the most recent and YOLO-native option. Published in 2025 on arXiv (2504.07744) and available on HuggingFace (imageomics/mmla-project), it delivers **811,000 annotations** across 37 high-resolution videos from three sites: Ol Pejeta Conservancy and Mpala Research Centre in Kenya, plus The Wilds in Ohio. Species include plains zebra, Grévy's zebra, reticulated giraffe, Masai giraffe, Persian onager, and African wild dog — covering two of Eikelboom's three target species. The paper benchmarked YOLOv5m through YOLOv11m directly, with **fine-tuned YOLOv11m achieving 82% mAP50** versus 30% for COCO-pretrained baselines. This 52-point improvement quantifies the domain gap the user's fine-tuning strategy must bridge.

**Mara-Wildlife / WildlifeMapper** offers the broadest species coverage. Presented at CVPR 2024 by Kumar et al. from UCSB-VRL, it contains **11,151 images (8,400 × 5,500 pixels)** from Systematic Reconnaissance Flights over the **Masai Mara, Kenya**, covering **21 species** including all three of Eikelboom's targets (elephant, giraffe, zebra) plus wildebeest, buffalo, topi, impala, and 14 others. Tiled to 1,024 × 1,024, it yields ~77,000 training patches. The imagery is from a belly-mounted camera on a Partenavia P68 aircraft — not a drone, but nadir rather than oblique. Access requires approval from the UCSB-VRL lab via their GitHub repository (github.com/UCSB-VRL/WildlifeMapper), with a BisQue visualization tool available for preview.

**WAID (Wildlife Aerial Images from Drone)** provides the largest readily downloadable YOLO-format dataset. Mou et al. (2023) compiled **14,375 images** covering six species (zebra, cattle, camel, kiang, sheep, seal) in both nadir and oblique views. It is natively in **YOLO annotation format** with an 11,118/2,054/1,203 split. While not African-savanna-specific, its inclusion of zebras and diverse terrains (grassland, desert, coastal) builds robustness. The SE-YOLO paper showed that models trained on WAID maintained cross-dataset stability, whereas models trained on a heterogeneous multi-source compilation (MSAWID) collapsed to just **15.3% mAP on WAID** — demonstrating that dataset coherence matters more than raw diversity.

**African Ungulate and Gelada Dataset** (Koger et al., 2023) adds **40,532 bounding boxes** on 1,982 nadir drone images of zebra, gazelle, waterbuck, buffalo, gelada, and humans from Ethiopia and Kenya. Annotations are in COCO JSON format. This dataset is referenced as "BK-L23" in May et al. (2025) and was used alongside Eikelboom's data in that benchmarking study, making it a tested complement. Available from MPI's Edmond repository.

## Supplementary datasets worth considering

Several additional resources provide value for specific training phases or augmentation strategies:

- **Aerial Elephant Dataset (AED)**: 2,101 images with **15,511 elephant annotations** from South Africa, Botswana, and Namibia (Naudé & Joubert, CVPR-W 2019). Single-species but deep coverage of Eikelboom's highest-AP class. Note: annotations are point-based, not bounding boxes.
- **KABR (Kenyan Animal Behavior Recognition)**: 10+ hours of 5.4K-resolution drone video of giraffes and zebras at Mpala Research Centre, Kenya, with bounding box tracks (Kholiavchenko et al., WACV 2024). Available on HuggingFace (imageomics/KABR). Low altitude (10–50 m), very high resolution.
- **SAVMAP/Kuzikus**: The foundational Kellenberger et al. (2018) dataset from Kuzikus Wildlife Reserve, Namibia — nadir UAV at ~3 cm GSD with 20+ species. Available on Zenodo (10.5281/zenodo.1204408). Annotations are points only.
- **BIRDSAI / Conservation Drones**: Thermal infrared drone videos from Southern Africa on LILA BC, with bounding boxes for elephants, giraffes, humans, and other species. Useful only if incorporating thermal modality.
- **NOAA Arctic Seals 2019**: ~80,000 aerial images with ~28,000 bounding boxes. Not ecologically relevant but massive and useful for general aerial small-object detection pretraining.
- **AnimalDrone**: 53,644 images with 4 million dot annotations across 10 species including zebra and giraffe (Zhu et al., IEEE TIP 2021). Enormous scale but point annotations only.

## Literature points to domain-specific fine-tuning as non-negotiable

The research literature converges on several findings directly relevant to the user's 3-phase training strategy:

**The camera-trap-to-aerial domain gap is the central challenge.** MegaDetector (V5/V6) was trained on side-view camera trap imagery and performs poorly on top-down aerial perspectives. AnimalDetect.com testing (2025) confirmed: "These models aren't really designed for top-view or drone perspectives." The MMLA study quantified this — COCO-pretrained YOLO baselines scored **19–30% mAP50** at two of three sites before fine-tuning. This validates the user's approach of retaining MegaDetector's backbone knowledge while specializing the detection heads.

**Self-supervised pretraining on aerial imagery outperforms ImageNet features.** Zheng et al. (2021, ICCV Workshops) showed that MoCo+CLD pretraining on unlabeled aerial images achieved **double the recall** of ImageNet-pretrained baselines at equivalent precision, even with only 10% of annotations. For the user's frozen-backbone phase, this suggests that if possible, replacing or supplementing ImageNet/COCO features with self-supervised aerial features would yield substantial gains.

**Dataset diversity trumps raw size, but coherence matters.** WAID's cross-dataset experiment is instructive: training on the coherent WAID dataset maintained generalization, while training on MSAWID (a stitched-together multi-source compilation) collapsed to 15.3% mAP. This implies the user should carefully curate training mixtures rather than naively merging all available data. A recommended approach: use Liège + MMLA as the primary training mixture (both nadir UAV, African savanna) and add WAID or Mara-Wildlife as secondary diversity sources with careful class balancing.

**Active learning dramatically reduces annotation needs for new domains.** Kellenberger et al. (2019, IEEE TGRS) demonstrated that Transfer Sampling — an active learning criterion — enables effective model adaptation with only **0.5% of labels** from the target domain. If the user can annotate even a small subset of Eikelboom-style imagery, active learning can bridge the remaining domain gap efficiently.

No paper was found that systematically benchmarks specific training dataset combinations evaluated on the Eikelboom test set. The closest work is May et al. (2025), which tested YOLOv8 and HerdNet on Eikelboom (JE-TL19) alongside three other aerial datasets, finding that **object detection (YOLO) outperforms localization on nadir images and is competitive on oblique images** like Eikelboom's.

## Recommended training data strategy for the 3-phase pipeline

For the user's freeze-backbone → progressive-unfreeze → full-fine-tune approach with YOLO11l initialized from MegaDetector weights, the evidence supports this dataset combination:

- **Phase 1 (frozen backbone, train detection heads)**: Liège African Mammals + MMLA combined. These provide ~820,000 annotations of African savanna species in aerial perspective, sufficient to adapt detection heads from MegaDetector's camera-trap geometry to aerial geometry without destabilizing learned features. Remap species classes to MegaDetector's "animal" class for this phase.
- **Phase 2 (progressive unfreeze)**: Add WAID (14,375 images, YOLO-native) and the Koger African Ungulate dataset (40,532 boxes) for broader habitat and species variation. Begin unfreezing deeper backbone layers to adapt feature representations.
- **Phase 3 (full fine-tune)**: Include Eikelboom's own 393-image training split with its oblique-view imagery. If Mara-Wildlife access is approved, add it here for maximum species diversity. Apply aggressive geometric augmentation (rotation, flipping) validated by Zheng et al. as critical for aerial imagery. Consider copy-paste augmentation for small objects.

Key practical notes: WAID is the only dataset natively in YOLO format; Liège and Eikelboom use CSV, Koger uses COCO JSON, and MMLA uses YOLO-compatible format. All others require conversion. The comprehensive curated list at **github.com/agentmorris/agentmorrispublic/blob/main/drone-datasets.md** (also accessible via lila.science/aerialdata) maintained by Dan Morris provides standardized metadata and sample code for all listed datasets and should serve as the authoritative reference for download links and format details.

## Conclusion

The Eikelboom benchmark occupies an unusual position — oblique manned-aircraft imagery widely cited as a drone benchmark. This means training data should cover both nadir and oblique aerial perspectives. The strongest evidence-backed strategy combines the Liège African Mammals dataset for African species coverage, MMLA for YOLO-native Kenyan wildlife annotations at scale, WAID for cross-ecosystem robustness, and Eikelboom's own training split for domain alignment. The literature is unambiguous that aerial-domain fine-tuning is essential: MegaDetector's camera trap features provide a useful initialization, but the 20–50 point mAP gap between COCO-pretrained and aerial-fine-tuned models cannot be closed by architecture improvements alone. No existing paper directly optimizes training mixtures for the Eikelboom test set, representing a genuine research gap that the user's work could address.
